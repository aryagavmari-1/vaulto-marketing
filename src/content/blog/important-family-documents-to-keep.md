Most families don't have a documents problem until the day they do. A passport renewal, an insurance claim, a parent's hospital stay, settling an estate — suddenly someone is opening every drawer looking for one piece of paper, and no one is sure it still exists.

This is a practical checklist of the **important documents every family should keep** — what matters, **how long to keep each**, and **where to store them** so the right person can reach them when it counts. It's built to be skimmed, sorted in an afternoon, and kept up in a few minutes a year.

> This is general information to help you get organised, not legal, tax, or financial advice. Retention periods vary by country and change over time — check your local rules and confirm specifics with a qualified solicitor, accountant, or adviser.

## Why a documents list beats a drawer of paper

A drawer full of paper feels organised. It isn't — not in the moment that matters. The problem is never that the documents don't exist; it's that no one can *find* them, or know whether what they've found is the current version.

A **documents list** fixes the findability problem. Instead of a pile, you have a short, categorised record of what you keep and where each item lives. The paper can stay exactly where it is — in a folder, a safe, a solicitor's office — as long as one trusted place answers the only question that matters in a hurry: *what do we have, and where is it?*

That shift, from a drawer to a list, is the whole point. It's the difference between your family guessing and your family knowing. (If you've already started a [family asset inventory](/blog/what-is-a-family-asset-inventory/), this is its paperwork half: the assets list says what you own, the documents list says where the proof lives.)

## The important documents to keep, by category

Here's the core checklist. You won't have every item, and that's fine — work category by category and note where each one lives as you go.

### Identity and legal

The documents that prove who you are and what you've decided.

- Birth certificates
- Marriage, civil partnership, or divorce certificates
- Passports and national ID cards
- Social Security / national insurance numbers
- Wills and any letters of wishes
- Power of attorney (financial and medical)
- Advance directives / living wills

### Property and assets

The documents that prove what you own.

- Property deeds and title documents
- Mortgage agreements and statements
- Vehicle titles and registration documents
- Valuations for jewellery, art, and antiques
- A home inventory of valuable possessions — see [how to make a home inventory](/blog/how-to-make-a-home-inventory/)

### Financial accounts

The documents that show where your money sits.

- Bank and savings account details and statements
- Investment and brokerage statements
- Retirement and pension statements
- Insurance policies (life, home, health, auto)
- Loan and credit agreements

### Income and tax

The records you'll need at tax time and beyond.

- Recent tax returns and assessments
- Pay records and end-of-year summaries
- Business ownership and partnership documents
- Receipts supporting deductions or claims

### Health

The records that matter in a medical moment.

- Insurance cards and policy numbers
- Key medical records and an allergy/medication list
- Vaccination records
- Advance directives (again — keep a copy with health records)

### Digital life

The part most families forget, and the hardest to reconstruct.

- A list of important online accounts (email, banking, utilities)
- Access to your password manager
- Notes on digital assets — photos, domains, anything of value

The digital category is where families get most stuck after a loss, because nothing is on paper to find. Our [digital estate-planning checklist](/blog/digital-estate-planning-checklist/) is the companion piece for "if something happens" — read it alongside this one.

## How long to keep each document

Keep-everything-forever isn't a plan; it's a different kind of clutter. Some documents are permanent keepers, and some can go after a window. The table below is a **directional rule of thumb** — retention periods genuinely vary by country, so treat it as a starting point and confirm your local rules before you shred.

| Document | Rough rule of thumb |
| --- | --- |
| Tax returns + supporting records | Several years (often 3–7, varies by country); some keep returns permanently |
| Bank & credit card statements | ~1 year, longer if needed for tax or a warranty |
| Pay records / payslips | Until checked against your annual tax summary |
| Investment & retirement statements | Keep annual summaries; details until you sell + the tax period after |
| Insurance policies | While active, plus a period after any claim |
| Loan & credit agreements | Until repaid, plus a few years |
| Property deeds & mortgage docs | While you own the property, plus a few years after sale |
| Vehicle titles | While you own the vehicle |
| Wills, POA, advance directives | Permanent — keep only the current version |
| Birth / marriage / death certificates, passports, IDs | Permanent |
| Key medical records | Long-term to permanent |

When you replace a document — a new will, an updated policy — keep only the current version and clearly retire the old one. Two wills in a drawer is worse than none.

## Where to store documents so they're safe AND findable

This is the part everyone gets half-right. Storage has two failure modes, and most solutions only avoid one:

- **A fireproof box nobody can open.** Perfectly private, completely useless if your partner or executor can't get to it.
- **A shared link anyone can find.** Perfectly findable, and a privacy problem waiting to happen.

The safe-and-findable middle has four criteria. Storage should be:

1. **Private** — not on a public address, not in a shared email thread.
2. **Access-controlled** — *you* decide who can see what.
3. **Reachable by the right person** — your partner or executor can get to it when needed.
4. **Not a single losable copy** — one paper original in one drawer is one flood away from gone.

A few originals genuinely must stay paper — wills, some certificates, certain deeds are only valid in original form — so keep those secure and note *where* they live. For everything else, a clear digital record is easier to find and share with the right person.

## Make it a living system, not a one-time sort

A documents list goes stale the moment you finish it — a new account, a renewed policy, a moved file. The fix isn't more effort; it's a small rhythm:

- **Add as you go.** When a document arrives or changes, update the list then, not "later."
- **Review once a year.** A short annual pass — retire the old, add the new — keeps it trustworthy.
- **Don't make it one person's job.** The whole point is that someone else can find things. Invite your partner so the list isn't locked in one person's head (or one person's drawer).

That last point is the difference between a list that helps your family and one that dies with the person who made it. Bring in the people who'd actually need it.

## Frequently asked questions

### What documents should every family keep?

Keep the records that prove who you are, what you own, and what you've arranged. That means identity and legal documents (birth and marriage certificates, passports, IDs, wills, power of attorney, advance directives), property and asset records (deeds, titles, vehicle documents, a home inventory), financial and account statements, recent tax returns, key health records, and a list of your digital accounts. Group them by category and keep one current list of where each lives.

### How long should I keep financial documents?

As a directional rule: keep tax returns and their supporting records for several years (often three to seven, depending on your country), bank and card statements for about a year unless needed for tax or a warranty, and investment or property records until you sell plus the relevant tax period after. Documents like wills, certificates, and deeds are permanent keepers. Retention rules vary by country, so check your local guidance before shredding anything.

### Where is the safest place to keep important documents?

Somewhere private, access-controlled, and reachable by the person who'd need it — usually your partner or executor. The safest place balances two risks: a fireproof box nobody else can open, and a shared link anyone could find. Avoid a single losable copy. If you store documents online, choose a tool that keeps files behind access you control rather than on a public address.

### Should important documents be paper or digital?

Both, used deliberately. A few originals must stay paper — wills, some certificates, and deeds are often only valid in original form. For everything else, a clear digital copy is easier to find, share with the right person, and keep in one place. The practical approach is to keep originals safe and a digital record of what you have and where it lives.
