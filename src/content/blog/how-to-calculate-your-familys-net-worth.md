Net worth sounds like a term for the wealthy. It isn't. It's just one honest number that answers a useful question: *if we added up everything our family owns and subtracted what we owe, where would we stand?*

Knowing that number — and watching it over time — helps you make calmer decisions about your home, your savings, and your family's future. This guide walks you through calculating it, step by step.

## What "net worth" actually means

Your family's net worth is simple:

> **Net worth = everything you own (assets) − everything you owe (liabilities).**

That's the whole formula. The work isn't the math — it's gathering an honest list of both sides. Most families have never sat down and done it, which is exactly why the number is so clarifying the first time you see it.

A few things net worth is *not*:

- It's **not** your monthly budget. A budget tracks cash flow; net worth is a snapshot of what you have.
- It's **not** a credit score.
- It's **not** a judgment. A negative or modest number is just a starting point, not a verdict.

## Step 1: List what you own (assets)

Walk through these categories and write down everything that applies. Estimate where you have to — a rough figure beats a blank.

- **Property** — your home and any other real estate, at a realistic current value.
- **Vehicles** — cars, motorbikes, boats, at resale value (not what you paid).
- **Cash and savings** — current accounts, savings accounts, emergency funds.
- **Investments** — pensions, retirement accounts, stocks, funds.
- **Valuables** — jewelry, art, collections, and other high-value items.

Don't get stuck chasing exact numbers. A partial picture is useful. Mark a value as unknown if you're not sure, and refine it later.

## Step 2: List what you owe (liabilities)

Now the other side of the ledger:

- **Mortgage** — the outstanding balance, not the original loan.
- **Loans** — car loans, personal loans, student loans.
- **Credit cards** — current balances you carry.
- **Other debts** — anything else outstanding.

Listing what you owe isn't discouraging — it's what makes the final number *true*. A list of assets with no liabilities is a fantasy, not a net worth.

## Step 3: Do the subtraction

Add up the assets. Add up the liabilities. Subtract the second total from the first.

> Total assets − total liabilities = your family's net worth.

If the number is positive, that's the cushion your family stands on. If it's negative, you now know precisely where you are — which is the only place real progress starts.

You don't need to check daily. A simple rhythm keeps the number honest:
