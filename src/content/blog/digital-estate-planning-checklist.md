Estate planning has a reputation for being grim, complicated, and easy to put off. It doesn't have to be any of those things.

At its heart, estate planning is one act of care: making sure the people you love can find what they need, and know what to do, without you there to explain it. A will is part of that. But long before the legal documents, there's a more practical question — *is everything in one place your family could actually reach?*

This checklist covers exactly that. Work through it at your own pace. You don't have to finish in one sitting; getting it captured at all puts your family ahead of most.

## Why "one place" matters more than you'd think

When someone passes away or becomes unable to manage their affairs, the hardest part for families often isn't grief alone — it's the scavenger hunt. Which bank? Was there a pension? Where's the deed? Did they have life insurance, and with whom?

Every item that lives only in one person's memory becomes a question with no answer. A digital estate plan replaces the scavenger hunt with a clear, organized picture. That's the whole goal of the checklist below.

## The digital estate-planning checklist

### 1. The full asset picture
Your family can't manage what they can't find. Make sure these are inventoried:

- Property and real estate
- Bank and savings accounts
- Pensions, retirement, and investment accounts
- Vehicles and high-value belongings
- Any business interests

### 2. What you owe
Debts don't disappear; they get settled from the estate. List them so nothing surprises your family:

- Mortgage balance
- Loans and credit cards
- Any other outstanding obligations

### 3. The proof documents
For each asset and obligation, keep the document that proves it:

- The **will** and any trust documents
- **Property deeds** and titles
- **Insurance policies** (life, home, vehicle)
- **Account statements** and policy numbers
- Birth, marriage, and other certificates

### 4. The "what to do next" details
The context that turns a list into a plan:

- Who the executor or main contact is
- Where the original signed documents are kept
- Key advisors (solicitor, accountant, financial advisor) and how to reach them
- Funeral or care wishes, if you want them recorded

### 5. A way for the right people to reach it
A perfect plan nobody can access is no plan at all. Decide now who should be able to see the picture, and make sure it's stored somewhere they can be given access when the time comes.

## A few honest caveats

Estate planning touches law and money, so two things are worth saying plainly:

- **This is informational, not legal or financial advice.** A checklist gets you organized; a qualified solicitor or advisor makes it binding and right for your situation.
