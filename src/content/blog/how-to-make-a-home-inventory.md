A **home inventory** is a written record of the things you own — what they are, what they're worth, and where the proof lives. Most people never make one until they need it: after a flood, a burglary, or a death in the family. By then it's hard to remember what was in the spare room, let alone find the receipts.

This guide walks you through making a home inventory you can actually use — for an insurance claim, for your will, or simply so your family isn't left guessing. The goal is a calm, honest answer to one question: *if something happened, would the people who matter know what we own and where the records are?*

It takes an afternoon to start and a few minutes a month to keep up. You don't need special software to begin — though we'll show you a faster way near the end.

## Why a home inventory matters

A home inventory does two jobs that look similar but serve different readers.

**For insurance,** it's evidence. When you make a contents claim, your insurer asks what you lost, what it was worth, and what proves it. Reconstructing that from memory after the event is stressful and usually leaves money on the table. A list made *before* anything goes wrong turns a claim from a guessing game into a paperwork exercise.

**For a will or estate,** it's a map. An executor — often a grieving partner or adult child — has to find, value, and account for everything you owned. A clear **asset inventory for your will** spares them weeks of opening drawers and chasing institutions. It's one of the kindest pieces of admin you can leave behind. (Our [digital estate-planning checklist](/blog/digital-estate-planning-checklist/) covers the wider picture; this guide is the inventory piece of it.)

The same record serves both purposes. Build it once, keep it current, and you're covered either way.

## What a home inventory should include

A useful **home contents inventory** captures more than a name. For each item worth recording, note:

- **What it is** — a short, plain description ("Samsung 55-inch TV", not "telly").
- **Where it is** — the room or location, so an executor or loss adjuster can find it.
- **What it's worth** — an exact figure if you know it, or a sensible range if you don't.
- **Condition** — new, good, worn; this affects both claims and valuations.
- **Serial or model numbers** — for electronics, appliances, bikes, and tools.
- **Proof** — a photo, a receipt, a valuation, or the insurance policy that covers it.
- **Insurance status** — whether it's already covered, and under which policy.

You don't need to catalogue every teaspoon. Focus on what would be expensive, difficult, or emotionally costly to replace: electronics, jewellery, watches, art, furniture, appliances, tools, instruments, and collections. Then add the bigger picture — vehicles, property, and the documents that prove ownership.

## How to make a home inventory, step by step

### 1. Go room by room

Pick one room and finish it before moving on. Small wins beat an abandoned spreadsheet. Open cupboards and wardrobes — the expensive things are often the ones out of sight.

### 2. Photograph everything

A photo is the fastest proof there is. Take a wide shot of each room, then close-ups of valuable items and their serial numbers. Photos timestamp themselves and remove most arguments about whether you owned something.

### 3. Record the details that matter

For each item, jot down the description, location, rough value, condition, and any serial number. Keep receipts and valuations together with the item's record rather than in a shoebox you'll lose.

### 4. Note where the originals live

Proof of ownership often sits outside the house — the car's V5C, a property deed, a jewellery valuation, a share certificate. Your inventory should say *where* each original document is kept, even when you can't store the document itself.

### 5. Value sensibly

You don't need an appraiser for everything. Use recent purchase prices, current replacement cost for insurance, or a conservative estimate for an estate. Where an item is genuinely valuable — fine jewellery, art, antiques — a professional valuation is worth it, and you can attach it to the record.

### 6. Keep it current

An inventory is only as good as its last update. Add new purchases as they arrive and remove things you've sold or given away. A few minutes after a big buy is far easier than a full re-do every few years.

## Turning a home inventory into an asset inventory for your will

For insurance, contents are usually enough. For a will, your executor needs the whole financial picture — not just possessions, but accounts, property, pensions, and anything you owe.

That's the difference between a contents list and a full **asset inventory for a will**: the will version includes the assets that don't sit in a room. Bank and savings accounts, investments and pensions, property, vehicles, business interests, and the documents that prove each one — alongside the mortgages, loans, and cards on the other side of the ledger.

If you've already started a home inventory, you're most of the way there. Add the financial items, note where each proof lives, and you have a single record an executor can actually work from. ## How to value your items

Valuation trips people up, so keep two numbers in mind:

- **Replacement cost** — what it would cost to buy the item new today. This is what most contents insurers care about.
- **Market value** — what the item would sell for now. This is usually what matters for an estate.

For everyday items, a quick online check of the current price is fine. For valuables, get a written valuation and attach it. Recording *which* basis you used ("replacement" vs "market") saves confusion later — an item can be worth quite different amounts depending on who's asking.

## Where to keep your home inventory

A home inventory is sensitive: it's a list of your valuables and where they are. Three rules keep it both useful and private.

1. **Don't leave it next to the valuables.** A printout in the same drawer as the jewellery helps a burglar, not you. Keep the record somewhere separate from the things it describes.
2. **Make sure someone can reach it.** An inventory only your executor can't find is no inventory at all. Decide now who should be able to see it, and how.
3. **Control who can open the proof.** If you store photos and documents online, prefer a tool that doesn't leave files on a public address.

## Frequently asked questions

### What should a home inventory include?

For each valuable item: a plain description, its location, an approximate value, its condition, any serial or model number, and proof such as a photo or receipt. Note whether it's already insured and under which policy. Focus on things that would be expensive or difficult to replace rather than cataloguing everything you own.

### How do I value items for a home inventory?

Use *replacement cost* — what it would cost to buy new today — for insurance, and *market value* — what it would sell for now — for an estate. A quick price check is fine for everyday items; get a written valuation for genuinely valuable pieces like jewellery, art, or antiques, and keep it with the record.

### Do I need photos in my home inventory?

Photos aren't strictly required, but they're the fastest, most convincing proof you can have. Take a wide shot of each room plus close-ups of valuable items and their serial numbers. They make an insurance claim far easier to evidence and leave no doubt about what you owned.

### Where should I store my home inventory?

Somewhere private, separate from the valuables themselves, and reachable by the person who'd need it — usually your executor or partner. Avoid a single paper copy that can be lost or stolen, and if you store it online, choose a tool that keeps your files behind access that you control rather than on a public link.
