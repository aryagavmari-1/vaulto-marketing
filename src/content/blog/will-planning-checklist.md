> This is general information to help you get organized, not legal advice. Rules vary by country and region and change over time — check your local law or a qualified professional before acting.

Making a will is one of those jobs that feels enormous until you start — and the hardest part is usually not the will itself, but turning up prepared. This **will planning checklist** is the "before" step: everything to gather before you sit down with a solicitor or an online will service, so the will actually reflects what you own and who you'd want to decide.

Get this groundwork right and the appointment becomes short and calm — you answer the questions because you already have the answers. Get it wrong and you either pay for extra time or leave gaps your family discovers at the worst possible moment.

Here's **what to do before writing a will**, in the order that makes the actual meeting easiest.

## Why gathering comes before drafting

A will is only as good as the picture it's built on. A solicitor can word it perfectly, but they can only act on what you tell them. Forget the old pension from two jobs ago, or the savings account in just your name, and those assets don't vanish — they just become a puzzle your executor has to solve later, one letter to an institution at a time.

So the goal of this stage is one honest answer to *"what do we own, what do we owe, and who should decide?"* — written down before anyone drafts a clause. Do it once and the same record serves the will, a future review, and the people you leave behind.

## Your will planning checklist: what to gather

Work through these six areas. You don't need to finish them in one sitting — but you do want them all in one place before the appointment.

### 1. A clear picture of what you own — and owe

Start with the assets. List property, vehicles, bank and savings accounts, investments, pensions, business interests, and valuables — then the other side of the ledger: mortgages, loans, and outstanding cards. A will divides what's left *after* debts, so both halves matter.

If you've already made a home inventory, you're most of the way there. Our guide on [how to make a home inventory](/blog/how-to-make-a-home-inventory/) covers the possessions, and [what a family asset inventory is](/blog/what-is-a-family-asset-inventory/) covers the wider "what we own and owe" version that a will actually needs. Bring that list, not a vague memory of it.

### 2. The documents needed to make a will

This is where most people stall. The **documents needed to make a will** aren't exotic — they're the proof behind each asset on your list:

- Bank, savings, and investment statements
- Pension statements (including ones from past employers)
- Property deeds or recent mortgage statements
- Vehicle documents (such as the V5C)
- Life insurance policy details
- Loan and credit card statements
- Photo ID

You won't always hand these over — but the person making the will needs to know they exist, and your executor will one day need to *find* them. The quiet trap is location: the policy nobody knew about, the deed in a drawer nobody checks. So alongside each document, note **where the original lives**. A simple registry of "what it is and where it's kept" is often more valuable than the document itself.

### 3. The people: executors, guardians, beneficiaries

A will isn't only about assets — it's about people and roles. Before the meeting, decide:

- **Executors** — who you'd want to carry out the will. Pick people who are organised and willing, and check they're happy to do it.
- **Guardians** — if you have children under 18, who would care for them. This is often the single most important decision in the whole document.
- **Beneficiaries** — who inherits what, including any specific gifts or charities.

Write down your wishes here even if they're not final — a draft is far easier to refine than a blank page. (Vaulto can hold a note of these wishes so they're not scattered across your memory and a notebook, but it records your wishes; it doesn't assign or execute these roles — that's what the will and a qualified professional are for.)

### 4. Power of attorney: the decisions that can't wait

A will only takes effect after you die. A **power of attorney** covers the other risk: being alive but unable to make your own decisions, after an accident or illness. Many people arrange both together, and a short **power of attorney checklist** is worth running through before any appointment:

- Who would manage your **money and property** if you couldn't?
- Who would make **health and care** decisions on your behalf?
- Are those people willing — and do they know your wishes?
- Is there an existing arrangement that's now out of date?

These are uncomfortable questions, which is exactly why they're easier to answer calmly now than under pressure later.

### 5. Your digital life

Online banking, investment apps, photos, subscriptions, email, loyalty points — much of what we own now lives behind a login. None of it is obvious to an executor unless you make it so. Note the accounts that matter and how someone would responsibly find them (not a list of passwords in plain sight). Our [digital estate-planning checklist](/blog/digital-estate-planning-checklist/) walks through this part in full; for the will, the key is simply that your digital assets appear on the list at all.

### 6. Funeral wishes and the personal things

Not strictly part of the will's legal force, but the part families are most grateful for: any funeral preferences, letters, or notes on items with sentimental rather than monetary value. Gathering these now spares the people you love from guessing during the hardest week of their lives.

## Frequently asked questions

### What should I gather before writing a will?

A clear list of what you own and owe, the documents that prove each asset, and the people involved — who you want as executors, guardians for any children, and beneficiaries. Add details for anyone you'd want to make decisions for you through a power of attorney, plus your digital accounts and any funeral wishes. Having all of it in one place before you meet a solicitor or use an online will service means the will reflects your real situation, not a half-remembered version of it.

### What documents do I need to make a will?

Typically: proof of what you own (bank and savings details, investment and pension statements, property deeds or mortgage statements, vehicle documents), records of what you owe (loans, cards, mortgage), and identification. You don't always need the original documents to start — but you do need to know where each original lives, so your executor can find it later. Rules vary by country and region, so confirm the specifics with a qualified professional.

### Do I need a power of attorney as well as a will?

They do different jobs. A will takes effect after you die; a power of attorney lets someone you trust make decisions while you're alive but unable to — for example after an accident or illness. Many people set up both at the same time. A short power of attorney checklist — who would handle money, who would handle health and care, and whether they know your wishes — is worth working through before any appointment.
