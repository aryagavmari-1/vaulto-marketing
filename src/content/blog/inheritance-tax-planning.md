**Inheritance tax planning** has a reputation for being complicated, expensive, and best left until later. Most of it isn't. The hardest part is usually the simplest: knowing what you actually own, what it's worth, and which bits of it the tax might touch. This guide covers the inheritance tax planning basics in plain terms — what to gather, how an estate is valued, and the honest first steps — so you can plan properly without pretending to be an expert.

Inheritance and estate tax rules vary widely by country and region, and they change over time — check your local rules or speak to a qualified professional. This guide is UK-first, because the rules are specific to where you live. If you're outside the UK the *approach* still holds — value the estate, find what's taxable, gather the proof — but the thresholds and reliefs will be different, so treat the figures here as illustration, not instruction.

## Start with what you own, not the tax

Here's the part most articles skip: you cannot estimate an inheritance tax bill until you know what's in the estate. The tax is charged on the value of everything you own — so the real first step in inheritance tax planning isn't a clever scheme, it's a complete list.

That list is an **asset inventory**: property, savings and investments, pensions, vehicles, valuable possessions and business interests on one side; mortgages, loans and other debts on the other. The gap between the two is roughly your estate value — the number every later calculation depends on.

## How inheritance tax works in the UK

A quick, honest overview — not the whole tax code. In the UK, inheritance tax is generally charged at **40%** on the part of an estate that sits above a tax-free threshold. The headline numbers worth knowing:

- **The nil-rate band.** Each person has a tax-free allowance — commonly £325,000 — before inheritance tax applies. This threshold has been frozen for several years and is subject to change, so confirm the current figure.
- **The residence nil-rate band.** There's an additional allowance when your main home passes to direct descendants — children or grandchildren. It has its own rules and tapers away for larger estates.
- **Spouses and civil partners.** Transfers between spouses or civil partners are usually exempt, and any unused threshold can pass to the surviving partner — which is why couples are often assessed together.
- **The seven-year rule on gifts.** Gifts you make are generally free of inheritance tax if you live for seven years afterwards. Die within that window and they can be pulled back into the estate, with the tax on some gifts reduced on a sliding scale (taper relief).

Two estates of the same size can owe very different amounts depending on who inherits, when gifts were made, and which reliefs apply. That's why gathering the right information up front matters more than memorising rates.

## What to gather before you plan

This is the practical core. Before you can plan — or have a useful conversation with a solicitor or adviser — pull these together. Treat it as a checklist:

1. **The estate value — everything you own and owe.** Property, savings, investments, pensions, vehicles, possessions, business interests, and the debts against them. This is your starting number, and it's the one most people guess at.
2. **Your home, and who inherits it.** The current value, how it's owned (sole, joint, tenants in common), and whether it passes to direct descendants — because that changes which allowances apply.
3. **Gifts made in the last seven years.** Amounts, dates, and who received them. Significant gifts can still count towards the estate, so a simple record now saves your family guesswork later.
4. **Business and agricultural assets.** A business, shares in one, or farmland can qualify for specific reliefs — but only with the right evidence of what's owned and how it's used.
5. **Existing planning.** Any trusts, prior gifts, pensions with nominated beneficiaries, or arrangements already in place. Plans you've forgotten can quietly change the picture.
6. **Life insurance that could cover the bill.** Existing policies, their value, and crucially whether they're written in trust — because that determines whether the payout sits inside or outside your estate.

Gather these six and you've turned a vague worry into something you can actually plan around. Most of it is information you already have; it's just scattered across drawers, inboxes, and memory.

## How to reduce inheritance tax — the legitimate levers

There's no magic here, and anyone promising to "avoid" tax outright is selling something. But there are well-established, legitimate ways to reduce inheritance tax that are simply about using the rules as intended:

- **Use your allowances in full** — the nil-rate bands, the annual gift exemption, and small-gift and exemption rules that let you give within limits each year.
- **Spousal and civil-partner planning** — making sure unused thresholds pass between partners rather than being lost.
- **Gifting over time** — gifts that fall outside the estate once seven years have passed, where you can genuinely afford to give.
- **Charitable giving** — gifts to charity are exempt and can reduce the rate on the rest of the estate.
- **Life cover written in trust** — in the UK this usually keeps the payout outside your estate and gets it to beneficiaries faster, without waiting for probate. Many policies are never set up this way.

Which of these fit *you* depends on your numbers, your family, and your jurisdiction — and the order you do them in matters. This is exactly where a qualified solicitor, accountant, or financial adviser earns their fee. Your job before that conversation is to arrive with the facts gathered, so the time is spent on strategy rather than fact-finding.

## Frequently asked questions

### What do I need to gather for inheritance tax planning?

Start with a clear estate value — everything you own and owe — then your home and who inherits it, any significant gifts made in the last seven years, business or agricultural assets, and any existing planning such as trusts or life cover. You can't estimate a tax bill until you know what's in the estate, so a complete asset inventory is the genuine first step.

### How is the value of an estate worked out for inheritance tax?

The estate value is broadly the market value of everything you own at death — property, savings, investments, vehicles, possessions and business interests — minus debts such as a mortgage and reasonable funeral costs. Some assets attract reliefs and some gifts made before death are added back in, which is why a written record of what you own and owe makes the sum far easier.

### How can you reduce inheritance tax?

Common, legitimate levers in the UK include using your tax-free thresholds in full, spousal and civil-partner transfers, annual and small-gift exemptions, gifts that fall outside the estate after seven years, charitable giving, and writing life cover in trust so the payout sits outside the estate. The right mix depends entirely on your situation and where you live, so confirm the current rules with a qualified professional.
