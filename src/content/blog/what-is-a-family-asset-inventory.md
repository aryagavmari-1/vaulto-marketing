If something changed tomorrow — an illness, a move, a death in the family — could the people you love answer one simple question: *what do we own, and where is the proof?*

For most families the honest answer is "not easily." The details are scattered across banks, drawers, email inboxes, and one parent's memory. A family asset inventory fixes that. It turns the scattered picture into one clear list everyone can rely on.

This guide explains what a family asset inventory is, what to put in it, and how to start one this week — without building another spreadsheet.

## What is a family asset inventory?

A family asset inventory is a single, organized record of everything your household **owns** and **owes**, along with the documents that prove it.

Think of it as one clear picture made of three parts:

- **What you own** — property, accounts, investments, vehicles, and valuables.
- **What you owe** — the mortgage and any loans, so the list shows your *net* position.
- **The proof** — the deeds, policies, statements, and photos that back each item up.

It is not a budget and it is not a banking app. A budget tracks what you spend each month. An inventory answers a deeper question: *what does our family actually have, and could someone else find it if they needed to?*

## Why every family needs one

You don't need to be wealthy to benefit. You need a household with more than one person depending on it.

A good inventory gives you:

- **One source of truth.** No more "I think the policy is with the bank." It's in one place.
- **Readiness for life's big moments.** Buying a home, writing a will, helping aging parents, or settling an estate all start with knowing what exists.
- **Peace of mind.** The person who keeps the family's records in their head is one bad day away from being the family's single point of failure. An inventory removes that risk calmly.

## What to include in your inventory

Start broad, then add detail over time. A useful starter list covers five areas:

### 1. Property and big items
Your home, any other property, vehicles, and high-value belongings like jewelry, art, or collections.

### 2. Accounts and savings
Bank accounts, savings, pensions, and investments. You don't need exact balances on day one — naming the account is already progress.

### 3. Documents
Wills, deeds, insurance policies, certificates, and statements. These are the proof that turns a number into something your family can act on.

### 4. Obligations
The mortgage and any loans. Listing what you owe is what turns a pile of assets into an honest net-worth picture.

### 5. The people and details
Where each item lives, who else knows about it, and any account or reference numbers. This is the context that makes the list usable by someone other than you.

## Keep the proof, not just the number

A value with no evidence behind it is hard for anyone else to act on later. For each item, note **where the proof lives** — a document, a photo, or a serial number.

You don't have to track down every receipt to begin. A partial record is genuinely useful. List what you can, mark a value as unknown if you're not sure, and add evidence later. Captured beats perfect.
